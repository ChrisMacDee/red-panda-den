Building Clarence: Foam, Fleece & a Lot of Hot Glue

🎭

Clarence started life as a rough sketch on a Post-it note — a bear character in the style of Fozzie, built for a friend's comedy sketch project. What followed was six weeks of foam, fleece, hot glue, and more YouTube tutorials than I care to admit.

Materials & tools

For the head I used L200 foam — the closed-cell polyethylene stuff that Muppet builders swear by. It carves cleanly, holds its shape, and doesn't compress under fleece the way open-cell foam does. The body and arms are open-cell upholstery foam, which is softer and more flexible for movement.

Fleece for the outer skin. I went with an anti-pill fleece from a local haberdashery — it doesn't fray badly when you cut it, and it stretches enough to go over the foam curves without wrinkling.

The secret to good fleece work is cutting on the bias — at 45° to the grain — so it wraps curves without pulling.

Carving the head

The head started as a rough block, then I used an electric carving knife to cut the basic shape. This is the messiest part — L200 crumbles rather than cuts cleanly, and you end up covered in tiny foam beads.

From the rough shape, I refined with sandpaper and a Dremel. The mouth plate is a separate piece of thin L200, hinged with a strip of fleece so it opens and closes naturally when you move your hand inside.

Eyes

Ping-pong balls, painted with acrylic. The pupils are cut from black craft foam and glued on. The key is getting the pupils positioned slightly crossed — it reads as "cute" rather than "unnerving" on camera.

# Materials list for a basic hand & rod puppet head
L200 foam:     30cm × 30cm × 5cm block
Fleece:        50cm × 50cm (plus off-cuts for details)
Ping-pong balls: 2 × 40mm
Black craft foam: small sheet
Hot glue sticks: approximately one million

Skinning

Glueing fleece to foam is an art. The trick is to work in sections — don't try to cover the whole head at once. Start with the top, then the sides, then the jaw. Use a contact cement for the initial bond and hot glue for the edges.

The result

Clarence came out better than I expected for a first build. The mouth action is clean, the eyes have life, and — most importantly — my friend was delighted. He's now starring in three completed sketches and apparently has his own Instagram.
